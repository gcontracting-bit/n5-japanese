▶ 1. At a Glance

Feature English Spanish Japanese

Writing System Latin alphabet Latin alphabet Hiragana + Katakana + Kanji
Gender None Yes (masc/fem) None

▶ 2. Word Order: The Classic Example

Let's see how the same sentence structures across all three languages:

English (SVO): I eat sushi.

Spanish (SVO): Yo como sushi. (I eat sushi.)

Japanese (SOV): 私は寿司を食べます。(Watashi wa sushi wo tabemasu.)

Note for Spanish speakers: You already drop subjects ('Como sushi' works fine). Japanese does the same — it just moves the verb to the end. The particle system does the heavy lifting that word order provides in English.

▶ 3. Particles vs. Prepositions

Japanese uses particles (助詞 - joshi) instead of prepositions. They come AFTER the noun and define its role:

Role Particle English Spanish Japanese Example
Topic は (wa) — — 猫は (neko WA) — as for cats
Subject が (ga) — — 猫が (neko GA) — the cat (does)
Direct Object を (wo) — — 魚を食べる (sakana WO taberu) — eat fish
Location で (de) at / in en 公園で (kouen DE) — at the park
Direction に (ni) to a 学校に (gakkou NI) — to school
Time に (ni) at / on a / en 3時に (sanji NI) — at 3 o'clock
Means/Instrument で (de) by / with en / con 電車で (densha DE) — by train
Possession の (no) of / 's de 猫の本 (neko NO hon) — cat's book
Companion と (to) with con 友達と (tomodachi TO) — with a friend
From (origin) から (kara) from de / desde 東京から (Tokyo KARA) — from Tokyo
Until / Up to まで (made) until hasta 5時まで (goji MADE) — until 5 o'clock
Also / Too も (mo) also / too también 私も (watashi MO) — I also

▶ 4. Verbs and Conjugation

Japanese doesn't conjugate verbs by person. The same form works for I, you, he, she, we, they:

Tense English Spanish Japanese
Past I ate sushi Comí sushi 寿司を食べました (sushi wo tabemashita)
Present I eat sushi Como sushi 寿司を食べます (sushi wo tabemasu)
Negative I don't eat No como 食べません (tabemasen)
Past Negative I didn't eat No comí 食べませんでした (tabemasen deshita)
Major advantage: You don't need to memorize person variations (como, comes, comemos, coméis, comen). One form for all!

▶ 5. Nouns: No Gender, No Plurals, No Articles

Japanese simplifies nouns compared to both English and Spanish:

English:

a cat, cats, the cat, the cats

Spanish:

un gato, gatos, el gato, los gatos

Japanese:

猫 (neko) — "cat" covers all of these

Whether it's one cat, multiple cats, "the cat," or "a cat," you use the same word: 猫 (neko).

▶ 6. Dropping the Subject

Japanese, like Spanish, freely omits the subject when context is clear:

English: "I eat sushi." (subject required)

Spanish: "Como sushi." (subject optional)

Japanese: "寿司を食べます。" (Sushi wo tabemasu.) (subject optional)

For Spanish speakers: This is familiar territory! Japanese particles do the same job as Spanish verb endings — they clarify relationships even without an explicit subject.

▶ 7. Negation

Three different strategies across the languages:

English:

Helper verb + base form
"I don't eat"

Spanish:

"no" before the verb
"No como"

Japanese:

Verb ending changes
"食べません"
(tabemasen)

▶ 8. Forming Questions

Japanese has an easy rule: add か (ka) to the end of the sentence. No word order change needed:

Statement: 寿司を食べます。(Sushi wo tabemasu.) — "I eat sushi."

Question: 寿司を食べますか？(Sushi wo tabemasu ka?) — "Do you eat sushi?"

Compare: English requires word order change ("I eat sushi" → "Do I eat sushi?"). Spanish uses intonation or adds "¿" at the start. Japanese just adds a particle!

▶ 10. Your Bilingual Advantages

As an English-Spanish speaker, you already have mental frameworks that transfer to Japanese:

  • From Spanish: Subject-dropping comes naturally to you — Japanese does this too.
  • From Spanish: No future tense marker — Spanish "ir + a" or simple present handles it; Japanese does the same.
  • From Spanish: Verbs carry weight; word order is flexible — Japanese relies on particles instead.
  • From English: No grammatical gender — Japanese, like English, has no masculine/feminine nouns.
  • From English: No articles — Japanese is like this; you won't need to learn rules about "el/la/un/una".
  • Japanese bonuses: Consistent pronunciation — every kana and kanji sounds the same every time.
  • Japanese bonuses: No person conjugation — one verb form works for I/you/he/she/we/they.
  • Japanese bonuses: No plurals — never worry about singular vs. plural forms.

▶ 11. The Biggest Challenges

Be aware of these structural differences as you study:

  1. Word order (SOV): The verb comes at the end. This is the opposite of both English and Spanish.
  2. Three writing systems: Hiragana, katakana, and kanji — you'll need to learn all three to read fluently.
  3. は (wa) vs. が (ga): Both mark the subject, but they have subtle differences in meaning and use.
  4. Counter words: Japanese has different counters for different objects (3 books, 3 people, 3 flat things all use different counters).
  5. Te-form: A versatile verb form used for linking clauses and creating conditional/sequential meaning.
